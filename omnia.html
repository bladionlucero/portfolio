Introduction

Gym life should be smooth. And, yet, gym software - a cornerstone to its business - can cause unwanted hiccups along the way.

Why don’t athletes feel inclined to sign themselves into class? Why do they prefer to look at the workout on a website instead of the convenient mobile app? Why are coaches spending more time inputting a workout than being in the field? The answer to those questions lies in a product that streamlines all the essentials to bridge the gap of convenience and efficiency.

It boils down to a simple notion of time allocation: coaches coach and athletes...athlete (read: train, workout, etc.).

Learning from the Competition

Conducting a competitive analysis helped with a better understanding of how this idea could grow while learning from others’ pitfalls and successes.

Wodify
  • Wodify functions as an all-in-one. Attendance, performance data, point of sale, workout creation, kiosk functions, payroll, communication, documentation and more. It aims to be the sole, functioning software a gym would ever need. While it may have the largest clients, it is often described as “clunky,” “slow,” and “inefficient.”
Beyond The Whiteboard
  • BTW specializes in logging the workouts and then creating data reports to analyze athletic performance. It encourages social activity and quantifies fitness levels. It can be seen as half of a puzzle - a part the focuses more on the training and coaching side of a business.
MindBody
  • MindBody handles the financials - membership, inventory, payments, and the like. In many ways, it is used to complement Beyond The Whiteboard since it is more “behind-the-scenes” and business oriented.
User Research

While it is helpful that I have experience as both a coach and athlete, I focused my in-person interviews on those two main groups.

Major takeaways from the separate interviews include the following:

  • Coach
    • Would like to easily edit workouts, even if prior classes have already taken place in the day (this involves mobile)
    • Naming conventions of the workouts can be improved
    • Enjoys being able to see what movements have been recently programmed, preview mode also a nice feature
    • Mobile functions could use improvement
  • Athlete
    • Would like an easier way to sign in
    • Easier way to look up past performances, not have to calculate percentages
    • Logging workouts a hassle, many don't do it - even if the scores are written on the board
    • An incentive to workout
User Flow

From those interviews, I was able to designate a flow of how the app would operate, which would aid in designing the crucial screens for this showcase. Focusing on the main functionalities also allowed for consistent references.

The Beginning of Design

Beginning the visual process meant branching out from one word - an activity most everyone does inside the gym: workout.

The Brand

Omnia. A word that has stuck with me for a while. Well, actually, the phrase "carpe omnia" sticks - because why seize the day when you can have EVERYTHING? The “O” represents inclusiveness with a goal to reach all various forms of exercise.

OMNIA utilized a mostly blue scheme with orange highlights to contrast the dark, uninviting combo of red and black.

Revision, Revision, Revision

As the first project in my unfamiliar understanding of design, OMNIA underwent several revisions. From the initial wireframes to finished product, the difference became night and day.

Questions were answered:

  • How do we effectively create a workout?
  • How should we present an athlete’s information and accomplishments?
  • Or even how to notify an athlete to sign-in?
Final Mockups

While the focus was a singular page for a product showcase, the screens held a large importance, along with their placement. Other vital parts to the showcase - informational videos and partnered gyms - were also implemented with the final layout.

Putting It All Together

OMNIA culminates in a page showcasing a highly mobile-friendly app that simplifies multiple facets of the gym process.

Reflection

As with every project, there are victories to be celebrated and lessons to be learned from.

  • Plan First, Create Later
    • I spent a lot of time jumping from point to point without a clear direction. It may have been nearly expected as a n00b, but it put a ton of hinderance the flow of the project.
  • Talk to People With Preparation, But Be Open to Organic Convo
    • Speaking with coaches and athletes that I’m familiar with seemed a little “stiff” and overly “formal” at first, especially since I wanted to stick to a certain process. However, it’s important that - while it is best to stay on track - there were other underlying issues uncovered only through organic conversation.
  • Ask Lots of Questions
    • But just enough that I don’t get completely derailed. By asking more questions, it was easier to identify the problem and create a hierarchy of what needed to be done first.
  • Stay the Course
    • While initially frustrated, confused, and overwhelmed (how it is with most new things in my life), it was vital that I take the feedback and grow from it. Being able to know why a decision was made and to defend said decision became a backbone that would carry into other projects.
